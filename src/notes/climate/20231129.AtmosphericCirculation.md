## Introduction to Atmospheric Circulation

- Atmospheric circulation is a large-scale air movement on the Earth's surface, a pathway to redistribute heat and water vapor. In other words, atmospheric circulation is driven by the uneven distribution of energy among the Earth's surface atmospheres.

- The uneven energy includes the uneven solar radiation at different latitudes, more at the equator, less at the poles. Atmospheric circulation mainly occurs in the troposphere, where temperature decreases with altitude, warm air rises, and cold air descends.

- Additionally, the Earth's rotation also has a significant impact on atmospheric circulation.

- The basic structure of atmospheric circulation is stable, but local weather systems are more random.

## Four Pressure Belts

- Pressure belts are essentially processes of atmospheric ascent or descent caused by different temperatures in the vertical direction.
- ==Equatorial Low-Pressure Belt==: In the equatorial region, rich in heat, warm air carrying abundant water vapor rises to form a low-pressure area.
- ==Subtropical High-Pressure Belt==: The moist warm air rising from the equatorial area ascends and spreads towards the north and south sides, drying and cooling as it rises, and begins to descend around 30° latitude, forming a high-pressure area.
- ==Polar High-Pressure Belt==: Opposite to the equatorial region, least heat, cold air descends, forming a high-pressure area.
- ==Temperate Low-Pressure Belt==: Air from the relatively hot, dry high-pressure atmosphere of the subtropical high-pressure belt and the cold, dry high-pressure atmosphere of the polar low-pressure belt meet in the temperate zone (around 60° latitude), where the rising warm air again forms a low-pressure area.
- Low-Pressure Maintenance Mechanism: The temperate low-pressure belt and the equatorial low-pressure belt are both formed by rising warm air, which deepens the low pressure as the cooling warm air condenses water vapor, releasing heat and warming the surrounding air.
- High-Pressure Maintenance Mechanism: The high-pressure belts are areas where the atmosphere moves downward, typically drier. As the air descends, it is compressed and heated, further lowering relative humidity. Descending air is more stable than the surrounding air, inhibiting convection and cloud formation, reducing precipitation, and further maintaining clear and dry weather.

## Three Wind Belts

- Wind belts are essentially processes of high-pressure atmospheres releasing pressure towards low-pressure areas, coupled with Earth's rotation, forming different wind directions.
- ==Trade Wind Belt==: Blowing from the subtropical high-pressure belts towards the equatorial low-pressure belts, as the Earth rotates from west to east, the linear velocity at 30° latitude is slower than at the equatorial regions, due to angular momentum conservation, forming easterly winds, specifically northeast winds in the northern hemisphere and southeast winds in the southern hemisphere.
- ==Westerlies Belt==: Blowing from the subtropical high-pressure belts towards the temperate low-pressure belts, the linear velocity at 30° latitude is faster than at 60° latitude, thus forming westerlies. Specifically, southwest winds in the northern hemisphere and northwest winds in the southern hemisphere.
- ==Polar Easterlies Belt==: Blowing from the polar high-pressure belts towards the temperate low-pressure belts, the linear velocity at 0° latitude is slower than at 60° latitude, thus forming easterly winds.

## Three Circulation Systems

- Viewing wind belts as horizontal atmospheric movements and pressure belts as vertical atmospheric movements, combined, they form atmospheric circulation.
- ==Hadley Circulation==: In the equatorial region, warm air rises (equatorial low-pressure belt), moves towards the poles, descends in the subtropical regions (subtropical high-pressure belt), and then moves from the subtropical high-pressure belts towards the equatorial low-pressure belts (trade wind belt), forming a closed loop. This circulation is the main reason for the high temperatures and heavy rainfall in the equatorial regions and the aridity in the subtropical regions.
- ==Polar Circulation==: Cold air descends in the polar regions (polar high-pressure belt), moves towards the equator (polar easterlies belt), rises near the temperate zones (temperate low-pressure belt), and the rising air in the temperate zones moves towards the north and south, forming a closed loop. This circulation is the main reason for the cold and dry climate of the polar regions and the variable climate of the temperate zones.
- ==Ferrel Circulation==: A secondary circulation situated between the Hadley and polar circulations. Warm air from the equatorial region descends in the subtropical regions (subtropical high-pressure belt) and moves towards the mid-latitudes (westerlies belt), where it meets the cold air from the high latitudes, rises (temperate low-pressure belt), and then moves towards the north and south, forming a closed loop.

## Impact of Seasonal Changes on Atmospheric Circulation

- Hadley Circulation expands to higher latitudes in the summer and contracts in the winter. Since the winter in the Northern Hemisphere is the summer in the Southern Hemisphere, the Hadley Circulation in the Northern Hemisphere extends to higher latitudes in the summer, while it is at lower latitudes in the Southern Hemisphere.
- Ferrel Circulation becomes more active in the winter, as the temperature difference between the polar and mid-latitude regions increases, leading to more storms and weather fluctuations in the temperate zones.
- Polar Circulation strengthens in the winter due to lower temperatures in the polar regions, having a greater impact on nearby areas.

## Local Atmospheric Circulation

- Atmospheric circulation is essentially caused by the uneven distribution of heat in the atmosphere. Therefore, wherever there is an imbalance in the atmosphere, there are circulations of varying scales.
- Urban Wind: Cities, due to human activities, emit a large amount of excess heat, creating a heat island effect compared to suburban areas. Warm air rises within the city forming a low-pressure area, then spreads outward, cools, and descends, forming a high-pressure area. Thus, winds blow from the high-pressure area to the low-pressure area within the city. If there is a heavily polluted industrial area within the urban circulation, it can blow pollutants towards the city.
- Valley Wind: In mountainous and adjacent valley areas, during the day, sunlight heats the mountains faster, making the air warmer and rising, while in the valleys it descends, forming high pressure. This is known as the valley wind. At night, the mountains cool faster, making the air colder than in the valleys. The warm air in the valleys rises and descends in the mountains, forming mountain winds. Therefore, it is more likely to rain in the mountains during the day and in the valleys at night.
- Plain-Mountain Wind: Similar to valley wind, during the day wind blows from the plains to the mountains, and at night from the mountains to the plains, but on a larger scale than valley wind.
- Sea-Land Breeze: Since the heat capacity of seawater is greater than that of land, the land heats up faster during the day, forming low pressure, and the wind blows from the sea to the land, known as the sea breeze. At night, the land cools faster, forming high pressure, and the wind blows from the land to the sea, known as the land breeze.

## Monsoon System

- The monsoon is also a large-scale atmospheric circulation system, with different heating levels of land and sea in winter and summer. In summer, the land is hotter, forming a low-pressure area, and the wind mostly blows from the sea to the land, bringing moist air and accompanied by heavy rainfall. Conversely, in winter, the sea is relatively warm, forming a low-pressure area, and the wind blows from the land to the sea, making the land relatively dry.

- The essence of atmospheric circulation is temperature difference, so in areas where the monsoon system has a greater impact, such as parts of Asia and Africa, the influence of the monsoon system will exceed that of the three major circulations.

- In Asia, the summer monsoon is mainly driven by the warm and humid air currents from the Indian Ocean, blowing towards the Asian continent, especially the Indian subcontinent, Southeast Asia, and southern China, bringing a large amount of rainfall. The summer monsoon is characterized by high temperatures and heavy rain. In contrast, in winter, the wind blows from the Eurasian continent to the Indian Ocean and the Pacific Ocean. The inland air is dry, so the winter monsoon brings less rainfall.

- In West Africa, the summer monsoon brings moisture from the Atlantic Ocean, while in winter the opposite occurs. East Africa is relatively complex, but there is also a pattern of wet summers and dry winters.

- The three major circulation systems also impact the monsoon system. For instance, the expansion of the Hadley Circulation during the summer increases the pressure difference between the equator and the subtropics, thereby intensifying the monsoon system.

## Impact of Global Climate Change on Atmospheric Circulation

- Heating of Polar Regions:
    - Global climate change causes the polar regions to heat up faster than other areas, weakening the temperature gradient between the poles and lower latitudes, leading to the weakening or instability of polar vortices and jet streams (Polar vortices are strong low-pressure areas present in the Earth's polar stratosphere, surrounded by strong cold air and high-speed westerly winds, also known as polar jet streams).
    - The weakening of polar vortices may allow cold air to escape more easily to lower latitudes, causing sudden outbreaks of cold air and increasing extreme weather events in mid-latitude regions during winter.
    - The weakening of polar jet streams may slow down the movement of weather systems, leading to longer periods of high or low temperatures in some regions.
- Increase in Extreme Weather Events:
    - Global climate change may cause major wind belts (such as trade winds and westerlies) to shift towards the poles.
    - Such changes may alter precipitation patterns, causing some regions to become drier or wetter.
    - Additionally, with more energy and water vapor stored in the atmosphere, the dynamics of weather systems are intensified. Extreme weather events (like heatwaves, droughts, heavy rains, and storms) may become more frequent and intense.
