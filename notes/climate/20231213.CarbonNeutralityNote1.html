                                            Part Two is Here: Understanding Carbon Neutrality: Notes Part 2

                                            # 1. How serious is global warming?

                                            • In 2020, the global temperature rose by 1.2°C compared to pre-industrial times, exceeding expectations.
                                            • For every 1°C rise in global average temperature, sea levels rise by more than 2 meters.
                                            • If the global average temperature rises by 2°C, 99% of coral reefs will disappear, massive permafrost will permanently thaw, and water resources will become extremely scarce.
                                            • Coral reefs cover only 0.27% of the total ocean area, but they are home to 25% of all marine species. The disappearance of coral reefs poses an extinction threat to these species and helps protect coastlines from storms and waves. The reduction of coral reefs could increase the risk of coastal erosion and sea level rise.
                                            • If the global average temperature increases by 5°C, the entire Earth's environment will be completely destroyed, with numerous cities submerged and widespread extinction of species.
                                            • The main goal of the Paris Agreement is to keep the temperature rise in the 21st century well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase even further to 1.5°C.

                                            # 2. What are the key points of the Paris Agreement?

                                            • Greenhouse Gas Emission Targets: The Paris Agreement requires participating countries to submit their Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs), which are their pledged greenhouse gas emission reduction targets.
                                            • Global Temperature Goal: The core goal of the agreement is to limit the global average temperature increase to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue limiting the increase to 1.5°C.
                                            • Review and Reporting Mechanisms: The agreement sets up a transparent framework requiring countries to regularly report their emission status and progress in implementing NDCs to ensure transparency and accountability.
                                            • Financial Support: Developed countries have committed to provide financial support to developing countries to help them reduce greenhouse gas emissions and enhance their ability to adapt to climate change.
                                            • Loss and Damage: The agreement acknowledges the loss and damage that might result from climate change and calls for support to help affected countries cope.
                                            • Global Stocktake: The agreement requires a global stocktake every five years to assess overall progress and to raise ambition for future targets.
                                            • Raising Ambition: Countries commit to revisiting and updating their NDCs every five years to continually increase the ambition of their emission reduction targets.

                                            # 3. Why is there a target for carbon neutrality?

                                            • Impact of Global Warming: See point 1.
                                            • International Agreements and Policies: Many countries have signed international agreements, such as the Paris Agreement, committing to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. These agreements aim to control the degree of global average temperature rise to minimize the adverse impacts of climate change.
                                            • Reducing Dependency on External Energy Sources: Currently, China's dependency on external fossil fuels is high, with oil dependency at 73% in 2020.
                                            • Promoting Global Industrial Chain Restructuring: Economic exchanges within the industrial chain no longer limited to traditional products and services, but also include carbon emissions at each link. Governments are also actively initiating research and pilot projects for carbon border taxes. These new perspectives and regulatory requirements will change the bargaining power of various parties within the existing industrial chain.
                                            • Economic and Social Impacts: Climate change has impacted agriculture, water resources, health, housing, and other areas. Achieving carbon neutrality can alleviate the pressure in these areas, promoting more sustainable and inclusive economic development.
                                            • Technological Advancements and Transformation: To achieve carbon neutrality, many countries and companies are investing in renewable energy and clean technologies. This not only helps reduce carbon emissions but also drives new technological development and economic structural transformation.
                                            • Creation of New Employment Opportunities: From 2020 to 2050, infrastructure investments of about 70 trillion yuan will be leveraged, accompanying various new businesses, creating over 30 million job opportunities in emerging fields like zero-carbon electricity, renewables, and hydrogen energy.

                                            # 4. Misconceptions about Carbon Neutrality?

                                            • Thinking that carbon neutrality is just an environmental issue. In reality, carbon neutrality involves multiple aspects of the economy and society, including policy, technology, finance, etc., making it a comprehensive issue.
                                            • Assuming that all companies involved in developing carbon neutrality-related technologies have promising futures. In fact, in addition to technological development, cost control is also a key factor in advancing carbon neutrality.
                                            • Believing that the national push for carbon neutrality will hinder economic growth and social development. However, carbon neutrality can act as a comprehensive strategy for economic and social development, beneficial for promoting sustainable development.
                                            • Thinking that transportation carbon neutrality and the complete electrification of roads can be achieved by not buying cars. In reality, transportation carbon neutrality requires a broader range of measures, including the decarbonization of public transportation and high-speed rail.
                                            • Believing that coal power units will completely exit. In fact, due to the uncertainty of wind power and other renewable energy sources, some coal power units need to be retained to meet stability needs.
                                            • Thinking that electrical power carbon neutrality only requires the use of clean power generation. Besides using renewable energy for power generation, it is also necessary to establish an electricity grid that can adapt to large-scale renewable energy generation, guide green electricity habits, and promote energy-saving production and living styles.

                                            # 5. China's Role in Global Carbon Neutrality Goals

                                            • Intensifying Carbon Reduction Efforts: China has announced more proactive measures to address climate change and fully embark on achieving carbon neutrality. This includes following the global trend of green and low-carbon development and actively participating in the new round of energy technology and industrial revolution.

                                            • 30-60 Dual Carbon Targets: China has proposed to peak carbon emissions by 2030 and achieve carbon neutrality by 2060. Despite significant challenges in energy conservation and emission reduction, China remains committed to contributing to the mitigation of further climate change. Additionally, achieving carbon neutrality will help China reduce dependence on external energy sources, transform political and diplomatic strategies, create job opportunities, and form a technological advantage.

                                            • Global Responsibility and Challenges: As the world's second-largest economy and the largest developing country, China faces the dual challenge of controlling total carbon dioxide emissions and maintaining stable economic growth. The Chinese government recognizes that achieving carbon neutrality is a major responsibility with significant domestic and international impacts.

                                            # 6. Long-Term Costs and Benefits of Carbon Neutrality

                                            • Balance of Short-Term Costs and Long-Term Benefits: The implementation of carbon neutrality may lead to increased costs in the short term, especially for low-carbon projects. However, in the long run, carbon neutrality can balance these short-term costs with long-term potential returns. As technology develops, the production possibility frontier in the low-carbon domain will expand, making long-term returns more attractive.
                                            • Distribution of Costs and Benefits: The distribution of long-term costs and benefits of carbon neutrality among different social participants (such as capital and labor) and its short-term distribution depends largely on the design of related mechanisms. For instance, the auction mechanisms for emission rights or quotas in carbon emission trading markets affect this distribution.
                                            • Transformation of Society and Economy: Carbon neutrality represents a new understanding of the nature and potential of energy. To achieve it, continuous investment in low-carbon emission reduction technology development, energy efficiency improvement, and power substitution is needed across various sectors. This may lead to additional 'green costs' for businesses and consumers in the short term, but it is a significant transformation of the entire social and economic structure in the long run.

                                            # 7. Challenges in Achieving Carbon Neutrality

                                            • Balancing Economic Development Needs with Energy Conservation and Emission Reduction: Achieving carbon neutrality requires finding a balance between economic development and energy conservation and emission reduction. For China, achieving carbon peaking to neutrality within 30 years is a challenge, while developed countries in Europe and America typically have a transition period of 50-70 years. Adjusting and optimizing the economic structure while achieving environmental protection is essential.
                                            • Challenges in Energy Transition Technologies: Transitioning to low-carbon and renewable energy technologies is key to achieving carbon neutrality. This requires technological innovation and large-scale application of new energy technologies, which may encounter technical difficulties and cost issues.
                                            • Difficulty of Enterprise Participation: For enterprises, achieving carbon neutrality means facing increasingly stringent carbon emission standards and increased carbon emission costs. Particularly for high-energy-consuming and high-emitting enterprises, developing low-carbon technology projects will face significant financing challenges, as these projects might require substantial initial investments and long construction periods with uncertain economic benefits.
                                            • Burden of Energy Conservation and Emission Reduction Actions: China started late in energy conservation and emission reduction, facing significant challenges and burdens. However, China still commits to achieving carbon neutrality by 2060.

                                            # 8. How to Achieve Carbon Neutrality

                                            • Comprehensive Collaborative Actions: Achieving carbon neutrality requires unprecedented large-scale cooperation. This involves not only governments of various countries but also enterprises, individuals, and the international community. For example, China's proposed 30-60 dual carbon targets.
                                            • Negative Emission Technologies: Merely reducing carbon emissions is insufficient for the substantial carbon elimination required. Therefore, a series of artificial technologies to increase carbon elimination and absorption, so-called "negative emissions," are needed. These technologies include carbon sinks, carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS), and direct air carbon capture, among others.
                                            • Transformation of Energy Supply and Demand Sides: On the energy supply side, the key lies in the substitution of clean energy as well as the transmission and storage of clean energy. On the demand side, carbon reduction can be achieved by improving energy efficiency and decarbonizing production and business processes, including the substitution of production materials, electrification transformation, and technological renovation of production operations.

                                            # 9. What are the advantages and disadvantages of China in achieving global carbon neutrality?

                                            • Disadvantages:

                                              • Late start, heavy burden: China started its efforts in energy conservation and emission reduction relatively late, facing greater challenges and pressures. Therefore, the process of achieving carbon neutrality is more difficult than imagined.

                                            • Advantages:

                                              • Reduced dependence on foreign energy: Achieving carbon neutrality helps China reduce its dependence on external energy sources.
                                              • Shift in political and diplomatic strategy: The realization of carbon neutrality goals may accompany a shift in China's political and diplomatic strategies.
                                              • Creation of job opportunities: The development of a low-carbon economy and related industries is expected to create new job opportunities.
                                              • Formation of technological advantages: Promoting the development of carbon neutrality-related technologies is beneficial for forming technological advantages on the international stage.

                                            # 10. How to assess the returns and risks of investment projects in the low-carbon industry?

                                            • Matching of capital supply and demand: The prosperity of the low-carbon industry depends not only on market demand but also on the availability of suitable capital. Mismatch of capital supply and demand can affect the completeness and consistency of the financial system.
                                            • Assessment of returns and risks: Investors need to assess the returns and risks of investment projects. This includes considering the economic, social, and environmental benefits of the project and seeking a balance among these aspects.
                                            • Sustainable development and investment return: Investment in the low-carbon industry needs to find a balance between sustainable development and considerable investment returns. Mechanism design plays an important role in this process, including how to incentivize and support the development of the low-carbon industry through policies and market mechanisms.
                                            • Policy support and incentive mechanisms: The decisions of policymakers in balancing economic, social, and environmental benefits have a significant impact on investment in the low-carbon industry. Effective policy support and incentive mechanisms can reduce investment risks and increase the attractiveness of investments.

                                            # 11. What is green finance? What can it bring us?

                                            • Diversity of green financial instruments: Green financial instruments include green credit, green bonds, and green equity investments, among others. These tools play an important role in the green finance system, helping businesses and projects obtain necessary financial support.
                                            • Role of green credit: Green credit is a major component of green finance, similar to personal housing loans or consumer loans. Financial institutions such as commercial banks, development banks, and policy banks provide investment loans and working capital for enterprises' new environmental protection projects, low-carbon projects, and green industries according to the national environmental economic policy and industrial policy.
                                            • Long-term value of green finance: Green finance is not only a financial innovation but also an important support for the transformation of the economic system and energy structure. Early involvement in the carbon neutrality market and the transformation of the energy system of the economy can reduce the cost of transformation and achieve a first-mover advantage. Green finance can improve returns, avoid risks, and promote social-level mechanism design.

                                            # 12. How can policymakers find a balance between economic benefits, social benefits, and environmental benefits?

                                            • Green economic transformation: Promote the transformation of traditional industries towards more environmentally friendly and sustainable directions, such as developing clean energy and promoting the application of energy-saving and emission-reduction technologies.
                                            • Policy and legal framework: Formulate and implement laws and regulations related to environmental protection, such as carbon emission trading systems, energy efficiency standards, and environmental tax policies.
                                            • Technological innovation: Encourage and support environmentally friendly technological innovations, including renewable energy technologies, carbon capture, and storage technologies, etc.
                                            • Public participation and education: Raise public awareness of climate change, promote environmental consciousness, and involve the public in carbon reduction actions through education and promotional activities.
                                            • International cooperation: Seek cooperation globally to jointly address the challenges of climate change. This includes technology transfer, financial support, and experience sharing.
                                            • Economic diversification: Reduce dependence on high-carbon industries, develop the service industry and other low-carbon industries, and achieve optimization and upgrading of the economic structure.
                                            • Social welfare and equity: Ensure that the interests of all social strata are balanced during the transformation process, especially the protection and support of low-income groups.

                                            # 13. What is CCUS? What are some technologies that are already in production and others that are still being researched?

                                            Definition: CCUS (Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage) is a climate change mitigation technology aimed at capturing carbon dioxide (CO2) generated in industrial processes or energy production, then using it for other purposes or storing it to reduce CO2 emissions in the atmosphere. CCUS technology involves several key steps: capture, transport, utilization, and storage.

                                            Technologies already in production use:

                                            • Carbon Dioxide Capture Technologies:
                                              • Post-combustion capture: Suitable for power plants and industrial emission sources (cement plants), capturing CO2 from flue gases using chemical absorbents.
                                              • Pre-combustion capture: Suitable for Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle (IGCC) power plants, converting fuel into a gaseous synthesis gas and then separating CO2 from it.
                                            • Carbon Dioxide Utilization Technologies:
                                              • Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR): Injecting CO2 into oil fields to help extract more crude oil.
                                              • Manufacturing carbonated beverages and other industrial products.
                                            • Carbon Dioxide Storage Technologies:
                                              • Geological storage: Injecting CO2 into underground rock formations, such as depleted oil and gas fields or deep saline formations.

                                            Technologies still under research:

                                            • Advanced Carbon Dioxide Capture Technologies:
                                              • Developing more efficient, lower-cost capture technologies, such as using novel absorbents or membrane technologies.
                                            • Broader Ways of Carbon Dioxide Utilization:
                                              • Converting CO2 into fuels, chemicals, or building materials, such as through chemical or biological processes to convert CO2 into usable carbon-based compounds.
                                              • Using CO2 for algae biofuel production.
                                            • Long-term Monitoring and Safety Research of Carbon Dioxide Storage:
                                              • Ensuring that long-term stored CO2 does not escape, researching the environmental impact and monitoring technologies of geological storage.

                                            # 14. What new types of job opportunities are brought by carbon neutrality?

                                            • Renewable Energy Industry: As reliance on fossil fuels decreases, the development and utilization of renewable energies such as solar, wind, hydro, and biomass will become important employment areas. This includes engineering design, installation, maintenance, and operation jobs.
                                            • Energy Efficiency Improvement: Fields such as building energy-saving retrofitting, production, and installation of high-efficiency energy equipment will create numerous job opportunities. For example, energy efficiency assessors, green building designers, installers, and maintenance workers for energy-saving equipment.
                                            • Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS) Technology: This is one of the key technologies for achieving carbon neutrality, involving a lot of R&D, engineering design, and operation management work.
                                            • Environmental Protection and Restoration: With the growing awareness of environmental protection, there is increasing demand for jobs in fields like ecological restoration, environmental monitoring, and protection.
                                            • Green Transportation and Electric Vehicle Industry: Including the production of electric vehicles, construction and maintenance of charging facilities, and planning and management of green transportation systems.
                                            • Circular Economy: Waste recycling, second-hand goods trading, resource regeneration, and other fields will offer new job opportunities.
                                            • Green Finance and Investment: With the increase in green investments and sustainable development projects, related positions in green finance such as risk assessors, sustainable development consultants will also increase.
                                            • Research and Education: There is also a significant need for professionals in R&D of carbon neutrality technologies, environmental education, and training.

                                            # 15. Carbon emission rights pricing methods?

                                            • Carbon Tax: A direct tax on carbon emissions, with the tax rate typically based on the amount of CO2 emissions per ton. This method is straightforward and can directly reflect the external cost of carbon emissions.
                                            • Emission Trading System (ETS, also known as the carbon trading market): Cap-and-trade mechanism: The government sets a total carbon emission cap and issues a corresponding number of carbon emission permits. Enterprises must hold enough permits to cover their carbon emissions. Carbon credit trading: Enterprises can buy or sell carbon credits on the market, with prices determined by market supply and demand.

                                            # 16. How to use market mechanisms to promote energy conservation and emission reduction?

                                            • Incentivizing the Development of Clean Energy: Provide tax incentives and subsidies to encourage investment in renewable energy projects. Incentivize companies to purchase or produce clean energy through mechanisms such as Renewable Energy Certificates (REC).
                                            • Promoting Energy Efficiency Improvement: Provide financial incentives or subsidies for technologies and products that improve energy efficiency. Implement energy efficiency standards and labeling systems to raise consumer awareness and demand for energy-efficient products.
                                            • Establishing and Improving the Carbon Trading Market: Develop carbon trading mechanisms to promote the rational formation of carbon prices, increase emission costs, and incentivize companies to reduce emissions. Gradually expand the coverage of the carbon market to include more industries and regions.
                                            • Innovative Financing Mechanisms: Provide financing support for low-carbon projects through green bonds, green funds, and other tools. Encourage the private sector to participate in the investment of low-carbon projects.
                                            • Transparency and Public Disclosure: Improve enterprise transparency and public awareness through carbon disclosure projects and environmental reporting requirements. Incentivize consumers to choose low-carbon products and services.
                                            • International Cooperation and Mechanisms: Participate in international carbon trading and emission reduction mechanisms, such as the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM). Share low-carbon technologies and experiences through international cooperation.
