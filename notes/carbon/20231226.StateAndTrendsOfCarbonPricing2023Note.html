                      # Introduction

                      • Unless otherwise stated, the data and updates in the report represent the situation as of April 1, 2023.

                      • Since the first "State and Trends" report was published 10 years ago, carbon taxes and emissions trading systems (ETS) have steadily developed. The proportion of global greenhouse gas emissions covered by carbon taxes and ETS has grown from 7% to about 23%.

                      # Summary

                      1. Global Challenges and Policy Responses: Nations are facing energy crises and high inflation, implementing relief measures such as adjusting energy taxes and subsidies. These measures have led to increased government debt levels. Climate action continues. Carbon pricing policies have become more complex.

                      2. Robust Performance of ETS and Carbon Taxes: Half of the ETS or carbon tax prices have risen, although actual growth is affected by inflation. A minority of governments have adjusted ETS or carbon tax policies due to the energy crisis. The number of implemented carbon pricing tools has increased to 73, covering about 23% of global emissions.

                      3. Record Revenues from ETS and Carbon Taxes: Revenues are close to 100 billion USD, with the main use being green spending (40%) and compensation for families or businesses (10%). Governments face trade-offs between different policy objectives such as increasing revenue, promoting community acceptance, and managing international competitiveness.

                      4. Rising Adoption of ETS and Carbon Taxes in Emerging Economies: High-income countries still dominate. Only one tool exists in the Middle East and Africa. Emerging economies are adopting carbon markets to mitigate climate change and manage transition risks.

                      5. Slowing Growth in the Carbon Credit Market: Compared to 2021, the issuance and retirement of carbon credits have slightly decreased. Market activity is mainly driven by voluntary corporate demand. However, compliance demand may become more important. Exchange-traded carbon credit prices have fallen, especially those from nature-based projects, primarily due to macroeconomic conditions and prominent criticisms of carbon credits and offsets.

                      6. Increasing Maturity and Diversification of the Carbon Credit Market: New investors and financial products are emerging. Initiatives are promoting market standardization and transparency. Progress in implementing Article 6 of the Paris Agreement, with countries signing bilateral cooperation agreements. (Note: Article 6 of the Paris Agreement relates to international cooperation in achieving emission reduction targets, especially through market and non-market mechanisms, providing a framework for the transfer of emission reduction outcomes between countries.)

                      # Chapter 1: Introduction

                      • Inflation and Cost of Living: The global energy crisis in 2022 exacerbated the inflation and cost of living crisis, especially in developing countries and among low-income populations. The post-COVID-19 economic recovery led to increased energy demand and prices, further heightened by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, which pushed fossil fuel prices up. Along with post-pandemic supply chain disruptions, this led to the highest level of global inflation in 30 years, forcing most countries to implement tighter monetary policies. By 2023, a further sharp decline in global economic growth is expected.

                      • Fossil Fuel Prices: Reached record highs, with governments worldwide taking measures to prioritize relief for households and businesses, especially concerning energy poverty. Developing countries faced the threat of a regression in electricity access, with many offering temporary energy price reliefs, including reductions or suspensions in fuel value-added taxes and other energy taxes, or direct aid to consumers.

                      • Debt in Developing Countries: The economic turmoil and repercussions of the pandemic in 2022 placed significant financial pressure on many governments. High expenditures during the pandemic and relief measures for the energy crisis exacerbated national budget burdens. Fossil fuel subsidies reached record levels in 2022, pushing the debt in developing countries to nearly 100 trillion USD (Note: The global GDP in 2022 was about 100 trillion USD). Declining tax revenues and higher interest rates made debt management even more challenging.

                      • Urgency of the Climate Crisis: In 2022, extreme weather events had severe impacts worldwide. Massive floods covered a third of Pakistan's territory, Europe, China, and India experienced extreme heat and droughts, and the Horn of Africa faced its worst drought on record, putting 22 million people at risk of famine.

                      • COP27: The 27th Conference of the Parties (COP27) to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change made progress in aiding vulnerable countries affected by climate disasters through the Loss and Damage Fund, as well as implementing Article 6 of the Paris Agreement. However, limited progress was made in discussions on phasing out fossil fuels and climate financing.

                      • Net Zero Commitments: By the end of 2022, 89 countries, including India, Australia, and Chile, accounting for 86% of global emissions, committed to achieving net zero emissions, with target dates ranging from 2035 to 2060. Many countries adopted market and regulatory incentives to support renewable energy and low-carbon technologies, such as the European Union's Emission Trading System and carbon tax policies. The U.S. took several steps through the Inflation Reduction Act, including a methane emission charge and subsidies for electric vehicles.

                      • Paris Agreement Goals Not Met: Despite some progress in climate policies, current efforts are insufficient to meet the goals of the Paris Agreement. Global collective efforts to prevent temperature rise above 1.5°C are falling short, with global warming potentially reaching 2.4°C to 2.6°C if current trends continue. To achieve the 1.5°C target, global emissions need to be reduced by 45% by 2030. While solutions are known and sufficient funds are available for investment, more practical action is required.

                      • Impact of Indirect Carbon Pricing: Beyond direct carbon pricing policies, there are widespread indirect carbon pricing signals, such as fuel consumption taxes and fossil fuel subsidies, especially significant in the context of the energy crisis. The scale of indirect carbon pricing significantly exceeds direct carbon pricing, with fossil fuel taxes and subsidies valued at over 1 trillion USD annually, compared to about 100 billion USD in revenue from direct carbon pricing policies (e.g., ETS and carbon tax) in 2022, and the voluntary carbon market's total annual value of approximately 2 billion USD. This indicates that indirect carbon pricing still plays a vital role in global carbon pricing.

                      # Chapter 2: Carbon Taxes and Emission Trading Systems (ETS)

                      • In 2022, the growth of prices in Emission Trading Systems (ETS) and carbon taxes slowed but showed resilience in the face of global energy crisis challenges. In these policy tools, half saw price increases, about a third remained unchanged, and less than 15% saw price decreases. The EU Emission Trading System (EU ETS) experienced significant price increases, surpassing 100 euros for the first time in March 2023. Despite increased market price volatility in 2022, several ETS, including South Korea's ETS, experienced price declines, with South Korea's falling by as much as 35%.

                      • A few countries temporarily relinquished carbon pricing policies in the face of economic challenges: Germany postponed a price increase in its national ETS for a year, South Africa extended its exemption of taxable emissions in its carbon tax, and Sweden also delayed a planned carbon tax increase. These measures aimed to mitigate the impact of soaring energy prices. Most jurisdictions maintained the ambition of their carbon taxes and ETS: some regions increased prices above the inflation rate (Note 1), such as carbon taxes in Ireland, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, and Norway. New Zealand and the EU continued to strengthen their ETS measures. However, other interventions to reduce energy prices affected the overall carbon pricing policy signal (Note 2).

                        • Note 1: In 2022, the global inflation rate neared 8.8%, creating a significant difference between nominal price changes (expressed in local currency) and real price changes. Colombia and Poland's carbon taxes, as well as the auction floor prices in California and Quebec's ETS, were adjusted for inflation, increasing by 5% plus the inflation rate annually. Germany's national ETS and carbon taxes in Canada, Chile, Singapore, and some European countries remained nominally fixed. In these cases, real growth was less than the defined nominal growth when prices were predetermined to increase by a certain value.
                        • Note 2: Fossil fuel subsidies were widespread, undermining the incentive effect of positive carbon prices. In 2022, many governments' energy policies aimed to support consumers and the economy but also reduced incentives for emission reductions. Although direct carbon pricing is expanding, fossil fuel subsidies and government interventions in energy pricing weakened the overall impact of carbon pricing. The International Energy Agency estimated that public spending to reduce energy bills globally amounted to 500 billion USD.

                      • Several countries and regions are strengthening their carbon taxes or ETS, marking a transition away from fossil fuels. Despite challenges, global commitment to carbon pricing policies continues to strengthen.

                      • Energy markets are the primary factor affecting most ETS prices, including limited natural gas supplies and high natural gas prices, as well as droughts in Europe, China, and the U.S. These factors led to a temporary increase in coal usage.

                      • To achieve the Paris Agreement goals, carbon prices must significantly increase in the long term. According to the High-Level Commission on Carbon Prices, by 2030, the carbon price should reach $61 to $122 per ton of CO2 (value in 2023). However, as of 2023, less than 5% of global emissions are covered by direct carbon pricing within this range (see below figure), and most high-price instruments are in Europe. The actual growth in carbon prices has not kept up with inflation, leading to a decrease in real value. The share of emissions incentivized by carbon prices varies significantly among countries, and policy features such as free allowances or rebates may limit the effectiveness of carbon prices in driving emission reductions. The overall strength of the carbon price signal depends not only on direct carbon prices but also on other policies and internal carbon pricing (pricing within organizations to manage climate-related business risks and prepare for the transition to climate neutrality).

                      • Due to the energy crisis, some countries and regions have experienced delays and reversals in implementing or expanding carbon taxes and ETS. Slovenia canceled its long-standing carbon tax, Indonesia delayed the introduction of a new carbon tax, and Austria and Germany respectively postponed the launch and expansion of their ETS. Additionally, despite Pennsylvania's plan to join RGGI, its implementation has been suspended due to legal challenges. These actions reflect the challenges carbon pricing policies face due to global energy market volatility.

                      • Despite some countries and regions expanding the scope of their carbon taxes or ETS or implementing new carbon pricing tools, the global percentage of greenhouse gas emissions covered by carbon pricing has grown only slightly, increasing to about 23%, less than a 1% increase compared to 12 months prior.

                      • In 2022, global government revenue from carbon taxes and ETS continued to grow, reaching a new high of nearly $95 billion. This growth is mainly attributed to a significant increase in EU ETS revenue.

                      • The EU ETS (which has grown 7 times since 2017) dominates in terms of government revenue, reflecting its scale, pricing, and evolution in quota allocation methods. The increase in revenue is partly due to price rises and a shift from free allocation to auction (about 35% of EU ETS quotas are still allocated for free, equivalent to $20 billion). In contrast, China's national ETS, while covering more emissions, generated no revenue due to its 100% free allocation. In South Korea, although the ETS covers a large volume of emissions with higher prices, the presence of free allowances and offsets makes its government revenue comparable to Mexico's carbon tax.

                      • The revenue from global carbon taxes and ETS is typically allocated for specific purposes, particularly funding green expenditures (46%), supporting affected households and businesses (10%), tax reductions (9%), and general budgets (29%). This designated use of revenue helps reduce political resistance and supports the implementation of climate policies.

                      • Carbon pricing revenue recycling through direct transfers has been implemented in multiple countries, aimed at alleviating the financial pressure of carbon pricing on low-income households. For instance, Canada, the EU, and Austria have adopted practices of returning carbon pricing revenue to households in the form of cash or tax reductions, especially for those whose living costs are affected. These measures aim to mitigate the negative economic impacts of carbon pricing.

                      • Most carbon taxes and ETS globally are concentrated in high-income countries, particularly in Europe and North America (at the sub-national level), but interest in carbon pricing is also growing in other regions. China's national ETS is the primary carbon pricing tool in East Asia and the Pacific, while in Latin America and the Caribbean and South Asia, although some countries have implemented carbon taxes, only Mexico has implemented an ETS. Carbon pricing tools are less used in Africa and the Middle East.

                      • The tax rates of carbon taxes and ETS prices in high-income countries are generally higher than those in middle-income countries. Most tools in high-income countries are priced above $50, with almost all above $15. In middle-income countries, most tools are priced below $10. However, there are also middle-income countries with tool prices above $10, such as the Beijing and Guangdong ETS pilot projects in China, Latvia's carbon tax, and Mexico's sub-national carbon tax.

                      • Developing countries face challenges in implementing carbon pricing policies due to the non-binding nature of the Paris Agreement on them and the lack of pressure to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Additionally, they confront specific social, economic, legal, and political barriers. Balancing increased energy access, reduced energy costs, and carbon emission reduction is difficult. Nevertheless, these countries are considering implementing carbon taxes or ETS.

                      • International cooperation on emission fees (minimum carbon price and policy coordination) is progressing slowly, with ongoing discussions but no concrete advancements. The G7 initiated a 'Climate Club', and members of the International Maritime Organization (IMO) have agreed in principle to levy a fee on global shipping CO2 emissions. The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) agreed to set the offsetting requirement baseline at 85% of 2019 emissions, applicable to the voluntary (2024-2026) and mandatory (2027-2035) phases where airlines must offset emissions exceeding this baseline.

                      # Chapter 3: Carbon Credit Market and Mechanisms

                      • The carbon credit market growth slowed in 2022, with a slight decline in supply and demand. Independent credit mechanisms still dominate, but CDM issuance surged. The market faces macroeconomic challenges and criticisms over carbon credit integrity, but continues to develop and mature.

                        • Note 1: Independent Credit Mechanism: Carbon credit systems created and managed by independent entities outside the international climate change framework, such as private sector or NGOs. They are typically operated by the private sector, offering flexibility but may lack uniform international regulation and standards.
                        • Note 2: CDM: A mechanism under the Kyoto Protocol allowing developed countries to invest in emission reduction projects in developing countries, earning carbon emission credits (Certified Emission Reductions, CERs).

                        Comment: In 2022, the carbon credit market contributed to a reduction of about 500 million tons of carbon emissions, compared to the annual 50 billion tons of emissions, accounting for 1%.

                      • More countries are establishing domestic credit mechanisms, often combined with ETS or carbon taxes, becoming sources of credit demand.

                      • Current carbon credit supply primarily focuses on renewable energy activities, accounting for 55% of credits issued in 2022. However, with the significant reduction in renewable energy costs over the past decade, these activities are often economically attractive without the additional income from carbon credits, thus not requiring the extra financial support from carbon credits.

                      • The supply of carbon credits from new large-scale renewable energy projects may decrease, with increasing focus shifting to nature-based activities, like agriculture, forestry, and land use (REDD). Although forestry and land use credits declined in 2022, they may see significant expansion in the future.

                        • Note 1: REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) is an international mechanism aimed at reducing greenhouse gas emissions through reducing deforestation and forest degradation.
                        • Note 2: Two issues with REDD+: 1) Jurisdictional emission leakage, where avoiding deforestation in one area leads to deforestation elsewhere, not actually reducing carbon emissions. 2) Overestimation of assumed deforestation levels in the absence of the project, leading to inflated emission avoidance calculations. Hence, the integrity of REDD credits themselves is criticized.

                      • Verra (the largest independent credit mechanism) saw a 243% increase in project registration and a 90% increase in verification applications in 2022 compared to 2021. The market influx of new entrants, but the scarcity of verification personnel, has slowed the registration and issuance of new projects. This bottleneck indicates potential for future growth.

                      • For the carbon credit market to support the deployment and expansion of technological carbon removal, a significant increase in carbon credit prices is necessary. The IPCC AR6 highlighted the need for large-scale technological removal of CO2 from the atmosphere to limit global temperature increases to 2°C or lower. Currently, the cost of direct air capture and storage of CO2 is estimated at $250 to $600 per ton, with current carbon credit market prices not stimulating the deployment of these technologies.

                      • Voluntary demand remains the primary driver of the carbon credit market, despite a recent decline. In 2022, corporate voluntary demand was about 196 million tons, a 140% and 70% increase compared to 2019 and 2020, significantly higher than compliance demand. Macroeconomic environment, bans on carbon credit tokenization, and enhanced scrutiny of offset integrity are reasons for the slowdown in voluntary demand.

                      • In 2022, most carbon credits came from renewable energy projects (52%). The significant decline in credits from forestry and land use projects (36% -> 23%) may be partly due to integrity issues. Credits from household appliances (including clean stoves) increased by over 30%, indicating a buyer preference for sustainable development projects over lower carbon prices.

                      • Many ETS and carbon taxes allow the use of domestic carbon credits, but with limits. For example, Singapore allows up to 5% of carbon credits for compliance by carbon-constrained companies. Countries with emission reduction targets needing to fulfill Kyoto Protocol second phase obligations may have additional demand for CERs, but the scale is not large.

                      • Carbon credit prices and trends vary between market sectors and project categories. The extent of decline differs by credit type, with nature-based credits experiencing the largest drop, from around $16 at their peak to less than $5 by year-end.

                        Note: The calculation of the standard price is not detailed; theoretically, the price differences across various carbon credit markets should be significant. This report provides data sourcesopen in new window

                      • Unlike quotas in ETS, carbon credits are inherently heterogeneous. They vary by underlying project type, issuing standard, year, etc., and prices between different credit types usually have significant differences.

                      • Futures contracts anticipate moderate increases in carbon credit prices in the coming years.

                      • Market diversity and complexity growth have boosted participation in the carbon credit market. New technologies have lowered entry barriers. The U.S. launched its first ETF focused on carbon credits, allowing investors to benefit from rising carbon credit prices without direct involvement in project development. These financial tools could serve as models for attracting larger-scale financing, including institutional investors.

                      • Investment growth and new financial models are driving carbon credit supply. Carbon service provider Abatable estimates that upstream investments for carbon credit generation exceeded $10 billion in 2022, a 40% increase from 2021. Project developers gain financing avenues. New tools like forward carbon tokens and emission reduction bonds support early financing.

                      • Exchanges are facilitating more carbon credit trading, with an increase in platform numbers. The world's largest carbon credit trading platform, Xpansiv CBL, saw a 6% decline in trading volume but a 44% increase in total value, reaching $795 million. Multiple exchanges listed new futures contracts, and new carbon trading platforms emerged globally.

                      • To expand the carbon credit trading market, greater standardization of carbon credits is necessary: providing reliable, transparent, and up-to-date benchmark prices. Clear pricing signals allow project developers to better assess investment opportunities and buyers to manage their price risks. However, the integrity and types of carbon credits also present challenges to standardization.

                      • Market size growth has sparked concerns over carbon offsetting, intensifying debates over carbon credit quality. Companies using carbon credits face accusations of 'greenwashing', necessitating cautious descriptions of offsetting activities (e.g., Shell Netherlands' advertising lawsuit loss in October 2022).

                      • Regulatory bodies are becoming more proactive in setting guidelines for company statements and the carbon credit market, mainly focusing on increasing transparency. In March 2022, the International Financial Reporting Standards Foundation required companies to disclose the extent of reliance on credits for climate goals and information needed to assess the integrity of used credits. In March 2023, the European Commission proposed a legal proposal to increase the transparency of environmental statements, including those related to carbon credits. Third-party rating agencies (BeZero Carbon, Calyx Global, and Sylvera) also offer project ratings.

                      • Note: Core Carbon Principles (CCPs) are proposed by the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) to assess and assure the quality of carbon credits. These principles are designed to ensure the transparency, reliability, and effectiveness of the carbon credit market. They include the following ten principles:

                        1. Effective Governance: Carbon credit projects must have effective governance to ensure transparency, accountability, continuous improvement, and overall quality of the carbon credits.
                        2. Tracking: Carbon credit projects should operate or use a registry system to uniquely identify, record, and track emission reduction activities and issued carbon credits, ensuring credits can be securely and distinctly identified.
                        3. Transparency: Carbon credit projects should provide comprehensive and transparent information about all certified emission reduction activities. Information should be publicly available in electronic format and easily accessible to non-expert audiences, allowing scrutiny of the reduction activities.
                        4. Robust Independent Third-Party Verification and Assurance: Carbon credit projects should have project-level requirements for robust independent third-party verification and assurance of emission reduction activities.
                        5. Additionality: Greenhouse gas (GHG) reductions or absorptions generated by emission reduction activities should be additional, meaning they would not occur without the stimulus of carbon credit income.
                        6. Permanence: GHG reductions or absorptions generated by emission reduction activities should be permanent, or in the case of reversal risks, measures should be in place to address these risks and compensate for reversals.
                        7. Robust Quantification of Reductions and Absorptions: GHG reductions or absorptions generated by emission reduction activities should be robustly quantified based on conservative methods, integrity, and scientific approaches.
                        8. No Double Counting: GHG reductions or absorptions generated by emission reduction activities should not be double-counted, meaning they can only be counted once to achieve a reduction goal or purpose. Double counting includes double issuance, double claiming, and double use.
                        9. Sustainable Development Benefits and Safeguards: Carbon credit projects should have clear guidelines, tools, and compliance procedures to ensure that emission reduction activities meet or exceed recognized industry best practices in social and environmental protection and generate positive sustainable development impacts.
                        10. Contribution to the Net-Zero Transition: Emission reduction activities should avoid locking in GHG emission levels, technologies, or high-carbon practices that are incompatible with achieving net-zero GHG emissions by mid-century.

                      • New carbon financial products are emerging, such as the carbon insurance offered by Kita Company, designed to protect buyers from the risk of pre-purchased carbon credits being non-interchangeable.

                      • Blockchain technology is being used to track carbon credit transactions or in the Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) process. For example, in December 2022, the World Bank, in conjunction with the Singapore government and the International Emissions Trading Association (IETA), launched the Climate Action Data Trust (CAD Trust), an open-source, decentralized global platform using blockchain's immutability for auditing, helping to detect issues like "double claiming." However, limitations are noted, specifically that input data must be reliable before entering the system.

                      • Tokenization of existing carbon credits grew rapidly but faced public criticism and concerns, leading to a subsequent ban. Between October 2021 and May 2022, Toucan Protocol bridged over 22 million carbon credits, accounting for more than 10% of the 2022 carbon credit market, after which major independent carbon credit organizations announced a ban in May and June. However, the consensus is that carbon credits should allow tokenization, and currently, Verra and three other organizations are developing best practice guidelines for tokenization.

                        • Note 1: Benefits of Carbon Credit Tokenization: 1) Increased transparency. 2) Reduced transaction costs. 3) Broader market scope. 4) Increased liquidity. 5) Enhanced financing avenues. 6) Improved tracking and verification of emission reduction outcomes.
                        • Note 2: Issues with Carbon Credit Tokenization: 1) Ignoring carbon credit quality. 2) Erroneous input data can compromise the system's credibility. 3) Disrupting market stability. 4) Potential double counting of the same emission reductions in different systems. 5) Compliance risks. 6) Greenwashing.

                      • Article 6.4 of the Paris Agreement introduces the concept of "mitigation contributions," referring to emission reductions generated through the 6.4 mechanism but not authorized for international compliance purposes, instead being used for climate financing, domestic carbon pricing programs, or voluntary actions.

                        • Note 1: The Paris Agreement was adopted in 2015 and took effect in 2020, focusing on enhancing nationally determined contributions (NDCs). The global climate governance focus shifted from the CDM mechanism under the Kyoto Protocol to the new mechanism 6.4 under the Paris Agreement: after 2020, emission reductions from CDM projects cannot be issued as CERs (to avoid double counting).
                        • Note 2: The biggest difference between Article 6.4 of the Paris Agreement and the CDM of the Kyoto Protocol is that the CDM mainly involved developed countries achieving their emission reduction targets by helping developing countries. In contrast, Article 6.4 aims to establish a global market for all countries to participate in, essentially using market mechanisms to promote emission reduction.

                      • Regarding the rulemaking of Article 6 of the Paris Agreement, much work remains, such as the authorization process, where a country agrees to adjust its purchased or sold credits correspondingly, and whether it can be revoked or amended later. The World Bank report suggests these issues may be resolved at COP 28.

                        • Note: As of COP 28, the rules for Article 6 of the Paris Agreement have not been finalized, with issues like the authorization process and revisions or revocations pending. Methodologies for the 6.4 supervisory body are also pending and are postponed to COP 29.
                        • TODO: More detailed reading of the original documents of the Paris Agreement and the Kyoto Protocol may be necessary.

                      • In the Kyoto Protocol, the CDM mechanism imposed few responsibilities on host countries. Under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement, more preparatory work is required, such as national legislation, institutional planning, record management of ITMOs (Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes), and MRV methods. Furthermore, each country under the Paris Agreement has its NDCs, and excessive transfers of ITMOs must be avoided to not compromise their NDC targets. Countries with limited experience in the Kyoto Protocol's carbon credit market lack the information and institutional capacity to participate in these markets.

                      # Chapter 4: Conclusion

                      • This report focuses on the development and trends of direct carbon pricing, covering three tools: carbon taxes, Emission Trading Systems (ETS), and carbon credit mechanisms. All are useful for incentivizing the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions, generating revenue, addressing local pollution issues, and attracting international finance. Specific approaches depend on the goals and policies of each jurisdiction.

                      • This year's trend shows that the coverage of carbon pricing is increasing, with rising prices, especially in carbon emission trading systems and carbon taxes. Indonesia, Australia, India, Mexico, and others are either optimizing their existing ETS or establishing new ones. However, under the impact of the energy crisis, there are also some exemptions or delays in policies, reducing the incentives of carbon pricing while alleviating the burden on the poor.

                      • In 2022, the carbon credit market continued to expand its coverage, but issuance volumes declined. The gradual transition to the operation under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement began. More bilateral agreements are being established, setting up frameworks and infrastructure to promote international cooperation. Interest in carbon credits continues, accompanied by urgent issues of integrity and quality.
